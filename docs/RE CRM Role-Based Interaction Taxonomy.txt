Real Estate CRM Role-Based Interaction Taxonomy
Role	Primary Goals	Allowed Actions	Data Visibility Boundaries	Collaboration Touchpoints	Explicit Exclusions
Realtor:	
Close transactions; Manage client relationships; Coordinate deal pipeline	Create/edit listings; Schedule showings; Generate offer documents; Track milestones; Communicate with all parties	Full visibility of own clients & listings; Limited view of other agents' active listings (MLS-style); No view of other agents' private notes or financials	Buyers/Sellers (primary), Mortgage Brokers (pre-approval, updates), Closing Attorneys (contracts, deadlines), Insurance Providers (bind dates)	Cannot view other agents' commission agreements; Cannot edit signed contracts; No direct subcontractor hiring without client approval
Buyer:	
Find suitable property; Secure financing; Close on purchase	Search listings; Save favorites; Schedule tours via agent; Submit offers electronically; Upload pre-approval docs; Sign documents digitally	Own saved properties; Own offer history; Property details (non-owner contact); Timeline of own transaction; No seller motivations or financials	Realtor (primary), Mortgage Broker (documentation), Closing Attorney (signings)	Cannot see seller contact info directly; No view of competing offers; No access to seller disclosure notes prior to contract
Seller:	
Maximize sale price; Minimize time on market; Coordinate move	Create/edit listing info; Review offers; Select offer; Schedule inspections/appraisals; Upload disclosures	Own property analytics; Offer details (price, contingencies); Buyer pre-approval status; No buyer full financial details	Realtor (primary), Closing Attorney (documents), Subcontractors (repairs, with agent coordination)	Cannot see buyer's personal financial details beyond pre-approval letter; No direct negotiation with buyer without agent
Closing Attorney:	
Ensure legal compliance; Facilitate title transfer; Manage escrow	Upload contracts; Add closing documents; Request signatures; Disburse funds; Record deeds	Contract documents; Title reports; Closing statements; Wire instructions; No party personal financial accounts beyond required for closing	Realtors (contract revisions), Mortgage Brokers (funding confirmation), Sellers/Buyers (signature requests)	Cannot disburse funds without authorization; No editing of purchase price or material terms; No access to early negotiation notes
Mortgage Broker / Finance:	
Secure loan approval; Meet closing deadlines	Upload/pre-populate loan applications; Request documents from buyer; Update approval status; Share closing cost estimates	Buyer financial documents; Credit reports; Appraisal reports; Loan estimate details; No seller financials unrelated to transaction	Realtor (approval updates), Buyer (document requests), Closing Attorney (funding details)	Cannot share buyer's full credit report with realtor; No direct contact with appraisers to influence value; No editing of contract terms
Insurance Providers:	
Bind policies; Ensure coverage deadlines	Upload quotes; Bind policies; Share certificates of insurance	Property details for quoting; Closing date; Buyer/seller contact info for policy; No financial details beyond premium	Realtor (coordination), Buyer/Seller (policy selection), Closing Attorney (proof of coverage)	Cannot access full property inspection reports unless provided; No marketing to other parties in transaction
Subcontractors:	
Complete tasks; Submit invoices; Coordinate access	View assigned work orders; Update task status; Upload completion photos; Submit invoices	Property address; Access instructions; Scope of work; No client contact info beyond necessary scheduling	Realtor (scheduling, approvals), Seller (access, if pre-authorized)	No access to transaction financials or documents; Cannot modify listing details; No direct client contact unless explicitly allowed
Narrative: How Role Isolation Prevents Complexity
A real estate transaction involves multiple stakeholders with competing sensitivities: financial privacy, negotiation leverage, legal compliance, and operational coordination. Without strict role isolation, the CRM becomes a tangled web where:

Information overload occurs when parties see irrelevant data (e.g., a subcontractor viewing loan details).

Privacy violations risk legal exposure (e.g., accidental sharing of financial documents).

Negotiation integrity is compromised if buyers see seller motivations or competing offers.

Accountability blurring happens when roles overstep (e.g., a mortgage broker editing contract terms).

This taxonomy enforces need-to-know visibility and action-based permissions. For example:

Buyers see listings and their own progress, but not the seller’s bottom line.

Realtors orchestrate but cannot access raw credit reports or disburse funds.

Attorneys see documents needed for closing but not early negotiation notes.

By isolating roles, the CRM reduces cognitive load, maintains transaction security, and ensures each party operates within their professional lane—turning a complex multi-party process into a streamlined, secure workflow where collaboration happens only at defined touchpoints with controlled data exchange. This prevents the system from becoming a chaotic, high-risk data free-for-all.
